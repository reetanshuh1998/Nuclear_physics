Abstract

Coulomb breakup reactions have been an important tool in unraveling the structure
of the exotic systems. This thesis concerns the application of the theory of elastic
Coulomb breakup of a projectile in the ﬁeld of a target nucleus under the framework
of ﬁnite range distorted wave Born approximation, to problems related to the structure and reactions of nuclei away from the line of stability. The analytic nature of
this theory stems from the fact that pure Coulomb wave functions are used in the
calculation and that the dynamics can be analytically evaluated.
The present endeavour is divided into seven chapters and three appendices.
Chapter 1 gives a brief introduction to the subject and presents a review of the
experimental scenario. Chapter 2 presents a short discussion of some of the theories
used to describe breakup reactions. In chapter 3, we investigate the breakdown of
N = 8 magic number near the neutron drip line by calculating the parallel momentum distribution of the charged fragment in the Coulomb breakup of various Be
isotopes (N = 5, 6, 7, 8) on Au target at 100 MeV/u. In chapter 4, we extend our
Coulomb breakup theory to include ‘deformation’ eﬀects in the structure part and
thereby extend the application of our fully ﬁnite range quantum mechanical theory
of Coulomb breakup to medium mass nuclei. Several reaction observables, like the
total cross section, relative energy spectra, parallel momentum distribution, angular
i

ii
and energy-angular distributions have been calculated. Comparing the calculated
cross sections with the available experimental data, an eﬀort has also been made
to put constraints on the large uncertainties in the one-neutron separation energy
of 31 Ne. In chapter 5, we have applied our Coulomb breakup theory as an indirect
approach in nuclear astrophysics. We calculated the 14 C(n, γ)15 C radiative capture
cross section and the associated reaction rate per mole as a function of temperature,
by studying the Coulomb dissociation of 15 C on Pb at 68 MeV/u. In chapter 6,
analytic eﬀorts in the direction of a post form breakup reaction theory with three
charged particles in the ﬁnal channel are considered. We have also calculated the
breakup amplitude with two charged particles in the ﬁnal channel, using our theory,
and have shown that it can be put on a ﬁrmer theoretical footing. The summary,
conclusions and future directions of our work are presented in chapter 7. Some
mathematical details of the formalism and of the approximations are also given in
various appendices.

Introduction

1.1

The prelude

Studies with stable nuclei have been the mainstay of our knowledge about nuclear
systems. However, this constitutes roughly ten percent of all the nuclei that are
supposed to exist. There are a large number of nuclei having very short half-lives
and very small one- or two-nucleon separation energies [1-12]. In other words, these
nuclei lie very close to drip lines (the limit of neutron or proton binding). It is
only in the recent past that advances in accelerator and related technologies have
provided us the opportunity to produce and work with short lived nuclei. In Fig.
1.1, (also known as the Segré chart), the neutron number (N) is plotted on the x-axis
and the proton number (Z) on the y-axis. The stable nuclei are shown by the black
squares. The positions of magic numbers are indicated by horizontal and vertical
bars. Above the valley of stability is the proton rich side (shown in deep pink) while
below it is the neutron rich side (shown in blue); these are limited by the proton
drip line (dashed red) and the neutron drip line (dashed blue), respectively. At
the drip lines the binding energy of the last proton(neutron), Bp (Bn ), is zero. The

proton drip line is reached quite fast because Coulomb repulsion is a hindrance to
the formation of proton rich nuclei. On the neutron rich side, however, there exist
a much larger number of nuclei. In both regions there lie a vast number of nuclei
which are yet to be explored. At the extremities of the nuclear chart, therefore one
has nuclei whose properties are seemingly diﬀerent from those encountered in the
valley of stability. It is indeed a challenge in many-body nuclear theory to construct
a microscopic theory of these unique quantum systems.

The ﬁeld of astrophysics is intrinsically connected to nuclear physics - being the
source of energy generation in stars and the cause of element formation. That being
the case, many of the paths of nucleosynthesis lie through nuclei which are far away
from the stability valley. Naturally, in many of these cases, where properties of these

1.2

Experimental facilities

The development of radioactive ion beam (RIB) facilities starts from the early eighties with the ﬁrst experiment performed at BEVELAC, Berkeley, by Tanihata and
his collaborators on 11 Li [13]. Since then a large number of RIB facilities has been
developed world wide. These include RIBF at RIKEN in Japan [14], FAIR at GSI
in Germany [15], FRIB at MSU in the USA [16], SPIRAL2 at GANIL in France [17],
ISOLDE in CERN [18], DRIBS at JINR in Dubna [19], ISAC at TRIUMF in Canada
and many others which have been, or will soon be, in operation in the world wide.
With these facilities, high intensity beams of large number of drip line nuclei can be
produced and accelerated.
These various facilities for producing RIB can be classiﬁed into two categories
depending upon the way of production of ion beams. One of these is the method
of projectile fragmentation or ﬁssion (PF) and in-ﬂight separation, and the other is
the Isotope Separation On-Line (ISOL) plus post acceleration [20].
In the PF method, “an energetic ion beam is fragmented after impinging on a thin
target, and the reaction products are subsequently transported to a secondary target
after mass, charge and momentum selections in a fragment separator”. Reasonably
intense, high energy beams can be produced by this method which are suitable
for nuclear reaction studies. Among the above mentioned facilities, this method
has been used at RIKEN-Japan, JINR-Dubna, GSI-Germany, GANIL-France and
MSU-USA to produce and study reactions induced by RIB.
In an ISOL type facility, “radioactive nuclei are produced by bombarding a
thick target with particles from a primary source and after ionization and selection

of speciﬁc masses by electromagnetic devices, they are post-accelerated to energies
optimized for the process to be studied”. In the ISOL method, very intense high
quality beams are produced, but with lower energies which are more suitable for
nuclear astrophysics and nuclear structure studies. The ISOL method has been
pioneered at the ISOLDE facility in CERN (Geneva) [21], but the ﬁrst ISOL type

1.3

Halo Nuclei

In some light drip line nuclei, a long low density tail of loosely bound one or two
nucleons have been observed. These nucleons appear as halo, surrounding the core
consisting the remaining nucleons. The properties of the remaining core has been
found to be the same as in case of normal nucleus. The concept of halo nucleus was
ﬁrst introduced by Hansen and Jonson [22] and this special feature was ﬁrst observed
in neutron rich nucleus 11 Li [13], where a large matter radius had been found through
the measurement of interaction cross section and then later on its two-neutron halo
structure had been conﬁrmed (see Fig. 1.2). Since then, many more one- and twoneutron halo nuclei had been identiﬁed. These include 11 Be [23], 14 B [24,25], 19 C [26],
the one-neutron halo and 6 He [27], 11 Li [13, 28], 14 Be [29, 30] and 17 B [29] the twoneutron halo nuclei. Along the proton rich side, some proton halo nuclei have also
been identiﬁed (8 B [31–33], 17 Ne [34], 20 Mg [35] and 26,27,28 P [36]). Unlike neutronhalo nuclei, in proton halo there exists a long range Coulomb repulsive interaction
between core and valence proton, in addition to the attractive nuclear interaction.

1.3.2

Coulomb Breakup

In most cases, halo systems have only “one bound state (the ground state) and
a broad featureless continuum”. Thus, methods of conventional nuclear structure
studies - measurement of energies and spin-parities of excited states are not immediately applicable in these cases. However, being loosely bound systems, they have
small binding energies, often less than an MeV. Therefore, these nuclei can be excited above their particle emission thresholds quite easily. Their breakup reactions
in the Coulomb and nuclear ﬁelds of a target nucleus, hence, are useful tools to investigate their structures. In fact, measurements of the breakup cross sections were
among the ﬁrst set of experiments performed with beams of the rare isotopes.
In Refs. [67–70], the total two-neutron removal cross section in the breakup of
11

Li from a number of target nuclei with beam energies ranging from 29 MeV/u to

800 MeV/u, has been measured. It was found that these cross sections increased
by decreasing beam energy, increased with target mass and for breakup from heavy
target like Pb, the major portion (> 75%) of the cross section comes from the
Coulomb breakup alone. On comparing with the cross section measured for the
breakup of 12 C on Pb at beam energy 1.05 GeV/u [71], it was found that Coulomb
breakup cross section for the drip line nucleus 11 Li are larger than the stable 12 C,
by almost two orders of magnitude. All these results suggest that the small binding
energies and large spatial extension of halo nuclei, lead to huge breakup cross sections
for halo nuclei. Large one-neutron removal cross section of 11 Be on Be, Ti and Au
targets at 41 MeV/u beam energy [72], also favour this.
The halo nuclei usually have large Coulomb breakup cross section, which is due
to the spatially decoupled nature of the halo that results in a direct breakup without
forming any intermediate resonances. Large cross sections have been observed at
low/intermediate excitation energies, which is due to characteristic low energies
dipole (soft dipole) response of the halo nuclei [73, 74]. This is in contrast with
the case of stable nuclei where giant dipole resonances located at high energies
exhaust the E1 strength. Furthermore, in the Coulomb (electromagnetic) excitation

1.3. Halo Nuclei

11

of one-neutron halo nuclei, the strength B(E1) varies inversely proportional to one
neutron separation energy (Sn ) [75]. Therefore, the peak position in the dB(E1)/dE
distribution shift towards low value of Sn and height of the peak also increases. Also
B(E1) varies directly proportional to rms radius of halo nuclei, so therefore in case
of halo nuclei due to their large radii, large B(E1) strength has been observed. One
can also use B(E1) distribution to ﬁnd the radial component of wave function of
valence neutron [76]. Soft E1 excitations have been also observed for two-neutron
halo nuclei [74], however, there is still a lack of full understanding (see Ref. [75]).
From the B(E1) distribution, one can obtain the relative energy spectra which is
nothing but variation of the cross section with respect to the relative energy between
the fragments in the ﬁnal channel. Similar to B(E1) distribution, the relative energy
spectra in the Coulomb breakup of halo nuclei is also characterized by a low energy
peak. The relative energy spectrum of the 10 Be + n system in the breakup of 11 Be
on a Pb target at 72 MeV/u, was measured at RIKEN [76]. A strong peak was
observed at about 800 keV excitation energy in this distribution, which is consistent
with the one-neutron removal energy of about 500 keV. A similar method was used
to measure the 18 C + n relative energy spectrum, in the breakup of 19 C on a Pb
target, at 67 MeV/u [77]. Here too, a peak in the cross section at low relative energy
was found.

1.3.3

Momentum distribution

The momentum distribution of the fragments in the breakup can also provide important information about the halo structure. According to Heisenberg’s uncertainty
principle, a narrow distribution in the momentum space leads to a large spatial expansion of the nuclear wave function in the coordinate space and vice-versa. Also in
direct reactions it is well known that the fragments still carry the information about
the projectile structure [78,79]. So, a measurement of the momentum distribution of
the fragments can provide the information of the density distribution in the projectile. Depending upon the direction with respect to the incident beam, one can ﬁnd
the parallel momentum distribution (PMD) (the momentum component parallel to
the beam direction) or transverse momentum distribution (TMD) (the momentum
component perpendicular to the beam direction) of either fragment. However, the
momentum distribution of the outgoing neutron(s) can be inﬂuenced by the ﬁnal
state interaction, but this is not the case with momentum distribution of residual
core.
The structure of the projectile is reﬂected more cleanly in the parallel (or longitu-

1.3. Halo Nuclei

13

dinal) momentum distribution, since the TMDs are aﬀected more by the fragmenttarget interaction [80, 81]. In the high energy approximation, the S-matrix for the
fragment-target system does not depend on the longitudinal coordinates and hence
the PMD is expected to be less aﬀected by the reaction mechanism [10, 82]. Classically it is clear that the PMD predominantly picks up contributions from forward
angles, while the TMD has greater contributions from the larger angles, where its interaction with the target nucleus are more important. So the TMD is more aﬀected
by the reaction mechanism than the PMD.
The (TMD) of the 9 Li fragment was measured in the breakup of 11 Li on 12 C and
deuteron targets at the beam energy of 790 MeV/u at BEVELAC, Berkeley, by the
Japanese group [83, 84]. The measured TMDs of the 9 Li fragment were best ﬁtted
by two Gaussians of diﬀerent widths. The narrow distribution had widths of (21±3)
MeV/c and (20 ± 3) MeV/c for the carbon and deuterium targets, respectively. The
width of the wider component was found to be (80 ± 4) MeV/c for both the targets.

of 11 Li on 9 Be, 58 Ni and 197 Au targets at the beam energy of 29 MeV/u [68] with a

were found to be very narrow [(24 ± 4) MeV/c]. A narrow width of TMD was also
observed in the breakup of other Halo nuclei like 11 Be, 6,8 He [85, 86], which shows
a consistency. However, on the other hand, the TMD of the single neutron in 9 Li
breakup reaction on 12 C target showed a broad Gaussian distribution with a width
of 120 MeV/c, which almost rules out a halo structure in 9 Li [86].

1.4

Change of magic numbers

The concept of magic numbers is one of the enduring features of the shell model.
Based, mostly, on the study of nuclei on or close to the stability line it was found
that if the neutron number (N) or the proton number (Z) was any of 2,8,20,28,50,82
or 126, the nucleus was exceedingly stable. However, over the last two decades, the
experiments performed with the nuclei lying far oﬀ the stability line have shown that
these magic numbers will disappear [98] as one approaches the proton or neutron
drip line and replaced by the new magic numbers. The concept of magic number
was borne out through studies of discontinuities in observables, such as, Q value of
the beta decay, the single nucleon separation energy, and excitation energy of the
ﬁrst excited state of an even-even nucleus [99].
For the nuclei at the shell closures, the ﬁrst 2+ state generally has a large excitation energy. However, in the β-decay spectroscopy of 32 Mg (N = 20) [100], a low
lying ﬁrst 2+ state suggests the disappearance of N = 20 in this nucleus. This was
also conﬁrmed in the Coulomb excitation experiment of 32 Mg performed by Moto+
bayashi et al. [101], where a large B(E2, 0+
1 → 21 ) value was extracted that further

indicated a deformed structure in 32 Mg. Similarly, a low lying ﬁrst 2+ state and its

1.5

Deformed halo nuclei

As mentioned in the above section, in case of neutron rich nuclei like 32 Mg, the signature of nuclear deformation has been observed through their low lying 2+ state and
large quadrupole transition parameter β2 . Among the halo nuclei, the well known
example is 11 Be nucleus, where parity inversion phenomenon has been observed. On
the basis of single particle shell model one can expect the last valence neutron to
occupy the 1p1/2 orbit in the ground state. However, the experimentally observed
ground state of 11 Be is 1/2+ . This suggests that in this nucleus 1p1/2 and 2s1/2
states are inverted in energy. In Refs. [107, 108], this parity inversion was explained
by considering the deformed structure of 11 Be.
Due to the low ℓ requirement, the halo structure often results from the anomalous
shell structure which further can be related to deformation or in another way, one can
say that the nuclear deformation increases the chances of halo formation in exotic
nuclei. Recently, some evidences of deformed halo structure has been observed in
31

Ne [109]. A large Coulomb breakup [40] and interaction cross section [109] of 31 Ne

clearly indicate a halo structure in it. Further, the large Coulomb breakup cross
section has been explained well by considering the valence neutron in 2p3/2 orbit
and not in 1f7/2 orbit [40] as suggested by the single particle shell model. A brief
review about this is given in chapter 4.
All these observations indicate that our current understanding of the NN eﬀective
interaction is limited, given that the knowledge is to a large extent based on studies
on stable systems. It is also being realized that the weak binding of nuclei close to
the drip lines, has a great eﬀect on nuclear properties, more so on the shell structure
of these nuclei. The shell structure is, of course, dependent on the average potential

both in form and shape and may have weak spin-orbit couplings. This might result

Brief review of theoretical models

2.1

Introduction

The theoretical origin of breakup reaction theory can be traced to construction of
the exact transition matrix (the T-matrix). This could be of the post (or prior)
form type, again depending on whether one has used the ﬁnal/post (or initial/prior)
channel form of the asymptotic Hamiltonian [1]. The post and the prior deﬁnitions
of the T-matrix are actually equal. As will be shown later in this chapter, that
the various approximations to the exact scattering wave function entering into the
T-matrix leads to diﬀerent breakup reaction theories like the distorted wave Born
approximation (DWBA) or the continuum discretized coupled channels (CDCC).

3.1

Introduction

The concept of magic numbers is one of the enduring features of the shell model.
It was borne out through the studies of discontinuities in observables, such as, Qvalue of beta decay, the single nucleon separation energy, and excitation energy of
the ﬁrst excited state of an even-even nucleus. However, over the last two decades,
the experiments performed with the nuclei lying far oﬀ the stability line have shown
that these magic numbers can disappear [1] as one approaches the proton or neutron
drip line. In some nuclei, especially near the drip lines, exceptionally low laying
ﬁrst 2+ excited state and large value of the reduced transition probability B(E2)
indicate the breakdown of magic number. This has been observed in neutron rich
32

Mg [2, 3] and 42 Si [4] isotopes, which indicate the breakdown of N = 20 and
43

using parallel momentum analysis
N = 28 shell closure, respectively. Nowadays people are more interested to look for
the breakdown of N = 8 magic number, which contain some interesting isotones of
light nuclei, like 10 He (unbound),11 Li (two neutron halo),16 O (doubly magic) and
22

Si. The breakdown of magic numbers is also related with the disappearance of

the shell gaps or the mixing of single particle orbitals, which are well separated in
stable isotopes. For example, the halo structure of 11 Be [5, 6] can be explained by
considering the valence neutron(s) in the intruder 1s1/2 orbit, that indicates the
breakdown of N = 8 shell closure.
Infact, the breakdown of magic numbers or disappearance of shell gaps has been
explained using the concept of “tensor force”, the most prominent spin-isospin interaction between proton and neutron spin orbit [1, 7, 8]. This interaction has been
found to be attractive for the protons in j< = l − 1/2 and neutrons in j> = l + 1/2
orbit (or vice-versa) and repulsive otherwise. However, the removal of protons from
the j< orbit, make neutron j> orbit less bound. This is the case with isotopes of
Be, where N = 8 shell gap (which according to shell model is formed between the
neutron 1p1/2 and 1d5/2 and nearby 2s1/2 ) disappear due to the rise of the 1p1/2 neutron orbit. The 1p1/2 moves so high in the sd shell that it ﬁnally results in inversion
between 1p1/2 and 2s1/2 orbit.

3.4

Conclusions

In conclusion, using Coulomb breakup reactions on a heavy target we studied the
breakdown of magic number N = 8 for the neutron rich Be isotopes using PMD of
the charged fragment. A relatively small FWHM of PMD of the core in the breakup
of 12 Be is an indication of the breakdown of N = 8 magic number. In contrast, N = 6
shows the signature of a magic number. Therefore, our hypothesis to use the PMD
in Coulomb breakup to study magic numbers can be used to predict breakdown and
emergence of new magic numbers in exotic nuclei in a simple manner. We have given
a detail of our fully quantum mechanical FRDWBA theory, which we will extend

Furthermore, for nuclei in this region, the inversion between normal-sd and intruderpf shell has been suggested [7], which also emphasizes the presence of deformed
structure in these nuclei. It would be interesting to study the reaction of these
neutron rich nuclei and the eﬀect of their deformed structure on diﬀerent reaction
observables. In this context, the 31 Ne nucleus (with N = 21) has attracted great
attention as it lies in the region of “island of inversion”. The theoretical calculations

4.1.1

Brief review of 31 Ne

At present, the 31 Ne nucleus is speculated to be the heaviest “halo nucleus”, which
also lies in the “island of inversion”. Further, there is a large uncertainty in its one
neutron separation energy (Sn = 0.29 ± 1.64 MeV) [10] and some controversies are
there over its ground state spin-parity. We shall discuss these in sub-section 4.3.1,
where we comment on the structure of 31 Ne.
The one-neutron removal cross section σ−n of 31 Ne on 12 C and 208 Pb targets
has been measured ﬁrst time by Nakamura et al. [11], at beam energy 230 MeV/u,
and the large value of Coulomb breakup cross section has been explained well by
considering the valence neutron in 2p3/2 (for Sn ≈ 0.4) or 2s1/2 (for Sn ≤ 0.8) orbit
and not in 1f7/2 orbit. Because of large Coulomb breakup cross section, small Sn
value and low angular momentum (ℓ) value of valence neutron, they suggest it to be a
halo nucleus. Recently, Takechi et al., [12,13] measured the interaction cross section
σI of 28−32 Ne on 12 C target at 240 MeV/u. They also explained the large value of
σI of 31 Ne by considering s- or p-wave valence neutron [13], which is consistent with
the experiment [11].
The ground state spin-parity (J π = 3/2− ) suggested by these experiments also
agree with the theoretical predictions of Refs. [8, 9]. Furthermore, the theoretical
calculations within Glauber and eikonal models [14] also suggest the ground state
spin parity of 31 Ne to be 3/2− by comparing the calculated value of σ−n with that
of experiment [11]. Therefore, one can expect the intruder conﬁguration and deformed structure in 31 Ne. In spite of this, one cannot neglect the possibility of 1/2+
ground state, as mentioned above, which can cause even more drastic changes to the
nuclear structure. To interpret the data [11] in terms of deformation, Hamamoto
did a Nilsson model calculation using a deformed Woods-Saxon potential [15] and
suggested that the ground state 1/2+ of 31 Ne comes from Nilsson level [200 1/2]
for Sn > 500 keV with quadrupole deformation parameter β2 ≥ 0.6, whereas 3/2−

4.3

Results and discussions

4.3.1

Structure of 31 Ne

31

Ne has a low one-neutron separation energy of Sn = 0.29 ± 1.64 MeV [10] or as

per the evaluated Nuclear Data Sheets 0.30 ± 1.6 MeV [27]. The ground state spin-

parity of 31 Ne has been quoted as 3/2− [8, 9, 18] or 1/2+ [19, 21] and therefore we
consider two conﬁgurations (i) a 2p3/2 neutron coupled to a 30 Ne(0+ ) core and (ii) a
2s1/2 neutron coupled to a 30 Ne(0+ ) core. In both cases, the single particle relative
motion wave function for the neutron is constructed by assuming a Woods-Saxon
interaction between the valence neutron and the charged core whose depth is adjusted to reproduce the binding energy. With the radius and diﬀuseness parameters
as 1.24 fm and 0.62 fm, respectively, the depths turn out to be 50.41 MeV and 29.88
MeV for two conﬁgurations mentioned above (with Sn = 0.29 MeV).

4.4

Conclusions

In this chapter, we have extended the quantal theory of Coulomb breakup within the
ambit of the FRDWBA to include deformations in projectiles in a simple manner.
The formalism retains the analytical ﬂavor of calculation in so much as the dynamics

4.4. Conclusions

83

part of the problem is still given by the Bremsstrahlung integral - which can be
analytically evaluated.
We have used the theory to investigate the breakup of 31 Ne on Pb and Au at 234
MeV/u. We have calculated the one-neutron removal cross section (compared it with
the available data), the relative energy spectra, the parallel momentum distribution,
angular and energy-angular distributions. In all these reaction observables we have
identiﬁed regions where the eﬀects of deformation of the projectile would play a
vital role. Our calculations, therefore, could also serve as a motivation for future
experiments with exotic nuclei in the deformed medium-mass region of the nuclear
chart.

5.1

Introduction

In the area of astrophysics there are a lot of interesting questions related to the
evolution of the universe, processes responsible for energy generation in stars and
how the nuclei that are observed on earth are formed. Particularly, one is concerned
with various processes like the CNO cycle, r-process, s-process through which elements are created. The knowledge of cross sections of some speciﬁc nuclear reactions
like capture reactions (p, γ), (n, γ), (α, γ) give important information about these
processes.
Although direct experiment are preferable but in large number of cases these are
diﬃcult. This is mainly due to the following reasons:
(i) These experiments have to be performed at stellar energies which are usually very small (of the order of tens or hundreds of keV/u), and the cross sections
for the reactions involving charged particles comes out very small (nano barns or
pico barns). Sometimes even after a long hours of data collection one obtains a
few events and further, background and stability problem has to be taken care of.
89

90

Chapter 5. Applications in nuclear astrophysics

These direct experiment at low energy can also be aﬀected by electron screening,
which also require proper treatment. Therefore, in such cases the alternate way is
to perform experiments at higher energy (some MeV/u) and then extrapolate the
results to down at required energy but this procedure further involves considerable
uncertainties.
(ii) Another problem is that many of the stellar reactions involve unstable nuclei
having very short life time.
So, to tackle this problem alternate indirect methods (combination of experimental and theoretical analysis) are being used where equivalent information can be
extracted by performing measurements at higher beam energies and these further
depends upon the type of reaction involved. There are three methods which are
popular as indirect methods in nuclear astrophysics.
(i) The Asymptotic Normalization Coeﬃcient (ANC) method [1]
(ii) The Trojan horse (TH) method [2]
(iii) The Coulomb dissociation method [3, 4]
In the subsequent subsection we shall brieﬂy discuss the Coulomb dissociation
method. Reviews of the ANC and the TH methods can also be found in Refs. [5]
and [6], respectively.

5.2

Overview of the 14C(n, γ)15C radiative capture
reaction

The radiative neutron capture (n, γ) process constitutes an important nucleosynthesis path in the neutron rich region of the nuclear chart. There are a very
few neutron capture reactions where both direct and indirect measurement of the
cross sections have been possible. The 14 C(n, γ)15 C radiative capture reaction is
one such example. As discussed by Wiescher et al. [7], it plays an important role
in the neutron induced CNO cycle, 12 C(n, γ)13 C(n, γ)14 C(n, γ)15 C(β − )15 N(n, γ)16 N
(β − )16 O(n, γ)17 O(n, α)14 C, which occurs in the helium burning layers of asymptotic
giant branch stars. Being the slowest in the cycle, its rate is important to predict
the abundance of 14 C given that it is one of the possible paths by which 14 C can
decay. Another importance of this reaction is in inhomogeneous big bang model [8],
which oﬀer the possible ways to bridge A = 8 gap and hence the production of
elements with A ≥ 12. The suggested reaction sequence in the neutron rich zone is
7

Li(n, γ)8 Li(α, n)11 B(n, γ)12 B(β − ) 12 C. Then the subsequent neutron capture over

12

C and 13 C leads to the production of 14 C, which has half life (5700 ± 30 years)

longer than the time scale of big bang nucleosynthesis. So, 14 C is considered to be
a seed nucleus for the production of heavier nuclei with A ≥ 20, where 14 C(n, γ)15 C
compete with alpha, proton and deuteron capture reactions on 14 C [9, 10].

Experimental eﬀorts to measure the 14 C(n, γ)15 C cross section via both the direct and indirect methods started during the early nineties. However, there are
disagreements in the extracted values of the cross sections, especially at very low
energies. The ﬁrst direct experiment to measure the (n, γ) cross section of 14 C was
performed by Beer et al. [11]. The extracted Maxwellian averaged capture cross section (MACS) was 1.72 ± 0.43 µb at 23.3 keV. This was very small as compared to

5.2. Overview of the 14 C(n, γ)15 C radiative capture reaction

93

MACS extracted from another direct experiment by Reifarth et al. [12], who found
it to be 7.1 ± 0.5 µb. Horváth et al. [13], extracted a value 2.6 ± 0.9 µb from the ex-

periment based on Coulomb breakup of 15 C on Pb at 35 MeV/u. Another Coulomb
breakup experiment was performed at GSI [14] at high energy (605 MeV/u) and the
extracted value of capture cross section was 4.1 ± 0.6 µb. Recently, Nakamura et
al. [15], also performed a Coulomb breakup experiment at 68 MeV/u beam energy
to calculate cross section of (n, γ) reaction. The MACS value extracted at 23.3 keV
was 6.1 ± 0.5 µb.
The theoretical calculations by Wiescher et al. [8], based on the direct capture
model [16] predict the value of capture cross section to be 5.1 µb. This value was in
good agreement as calculated from other theoretical models like folding model [17]
and the microscopic cluster model [18]. In Ref. [19], an indirect method based on
mirror symmetry was used to extract the (n, γ) cross section. Their value (5.3 ±
0.3 µb) was also consistent with that of Ref. [8]. Recently, a Continuum Discretized
Coupled Channel calculation [20] and also a semiclassical model based on the dynamical description of diﬀraction dissociation of weakly bound nuclei [21] have also
been reported to calculate the cross section of 14 C(n, γ)15 C.
With this background we present a fully quantum mechanical calculation of the
Coulomb breakup of 15 C on Pb at 68 MeV/u beam energy within the aegis of
post form ﬁnite range distorted wave Born approximation (FRDWBA) and then
extract the 14 C(n, γ)15 C radiative capture cross section and the reaction rate at
relevant stellar temperatures. The ﬁrst application [22] of this formalism to nuclear
astrophysics was on the Coulomb dissociation of 9 Li on heavy targets to calculate the
9

Li(n, γ)9 Li capture cross section. We would therefore like to apply the formalism

to the case of 14 C(n, γ)15 C for two reasons, ﬁrst to take advantage of the analytic
nature and secondly to bring into focus a slight discrepancy in the peak position of
the relative energy spectrum in the Coulomb breakup of 15 C at two diﬀerent beam
energies [14, 15].

Structure of 15 C

Conclusions

In conclusion, we have calculated the 14 C(n, γ)15 C radiative capture cross section
and the associated reaction rate by studying the Coulomb dissociation of 15 C on
Pb at 68 MeV/u, within the fully quantum mechanical post form distorted wave
Born approximation. The entire non-resonant continuum is accounted for in the
theory. We calculate the relative energy spectra in the breakup of 15 C on Pb at
68 MeV/u and discuss it in comparison with the available data [15]. However, one
must add that for a more universal application of our theory, especially at very
high beam energies, relativistic eﬀects need to be taken into consideration. The
local momentum approximation is also a price one has to pay to retain the analytic
nature of the dynamics part of the formalism. In fact, eﬀorts are on to overcome
this problem especially for neutron halo nuclei.
The neutron capture cross section is then calculated from the photodisintegration cross section of 15 C, using the principle of detailed balance. Comparison with
available data from both the direct and indirect measurements is done for a wide energy range. While our calculation is in good agreement with both data, nevertheless
we wish to point out that at Ec.m = 0.5 MeV, there are some disagreements between
the direct measurements and those extracted from the Coulomb dissociation data.
Since this is the region where the capture cross section is expected to be maximum,
it would be useful to experimentally investigate this region in more details.
We also calculate the 14 C(n, γ)15 C reaction rate per mole as a function of temperature. They are in good agreement with that evaluated from a direct measurement
of the capture cross section [12] especially for T9 < 1. Our results also show that
14

C(n, γ)15 C reaction will dominate over 14 C(p, γ)15 N for temperature T9 < 1.4.

6.1

Introduction

A quantum scattering theory, especially a breakup theory, with three charged particles in the ﬁnal channel is of interest in various branches of physics ranging from
the ionization of atoms and molecules by electron impact [1] to the breakup of a
projectile in the Coulomb ﬁeld of a heavy target nucleus [2], with implications in
astrophysics.
In Refs. [2,3] a ﬁrst order prior form distorted wave Born approximation (DWBA)
theory is used to study the eﬀect of three body ﬁnal state Coulomb interaction on the
breakup cross section for small scattering angle and relatively high energy. Further,
the ﬁnal state wave function was obtained by solving the Schrödinger equation in the
asymptotic limits, where it was assumed that the distance between the fragments is
much smaller than the distance between target and their center of mass.
Recently, solutions to the Coulomb scattering problem using the exterior complex
107

108

Chapter 6. Three charged particles in the final channel

scaling method [4] along with the potential splitting approach was proposed in Refs.
[5, 6]. Although the application of this method to three charged particles in the
ﬁnal state is still under progress as these are complicated, but it works well for the
case of two charged particles. The idea is to split the Coulomb potential at some
splitting point (R) into two parts - inner ﬁnite range part (r < R) and outer long
range part (r > R). Further, the solution of the outer part of the potential plays
the role of incident wave for scattering problem. The diﬀerence of the scattering
wave function and this incident wave results into a asymptotically behaving purely
outgoing wave, which satisfy the inhomogenous Schrödinger equation with a driving
term of ﬁnite range. Using external complex scaling one can reduce the asymptotic
boundary condition to zero boundary condition. The advantage of this method is
that the exact knowledge of Coulomb solution is not required. However, the choice
of splitting point R is crucial.
The Coulomb breakup problem has also been studied in Refs. [7–9], within the
surface integral formalism of quantum scattering theory . They generated more
general deﬁnitions of the scattering amplitude and hence the T-matrix in surface
integral form as compared to the conventional volume integral form. This formalism
is valid for both the short and long range (Coulomb) interactions. Further, without
any renormalization procedure they derive the post and prior form of the breakup
amplitude, which depends only on the asymptotic behavior of the wave function.
So, there is no need to know the behavior of wave function in the internal or nonasymptotic region. Further this surface integral form can be simply expanded in
partial waves. For the case of three charged particle the analytic form of three
body asymptotic wave function [10] was used. In Ref. [8] details of this method
along with the diﬀerent possible computational methods are given. The partial wave
expansion of the Coulomb distorted wave and dominant asymptotic expansions were
also studied in Ref. [11] and a three dimensional leading order asymptotic form of
the Coulomb distorted plane wave was derived, which further can be used in surface
integral approach [7–9].

6.1. Introduction

109

There are other theoretical methods in atomic physics used for three charged
particles, having their own limits of validity. These include variational approach [12],
convergent close-coupling [13], time-dependent close-coupling [14], R-matrix with
pseudo states [15] and Integro-diﬀerential close-coupling [16].
In this context, our eﬀort is to construct a post form theory with three charged
particles in the ﬁnal channel within the scope of the ﬁnite range distorted wave
Born approximation (FRDWBA). The inspiration being that in the problem of
breakup with three particles in the ﬁnal state, in which two are charged and one
uncharged, a factorization of the transition amplitude into two parts is possible [17] one containing the structure information of the projectile and the other involving the
dynamics of the scattering process. The latter part, involving full Coulomb distorted
waves, can be expressed analytically in terms of the Bremsstrahlung integral [18].
Given the analyticity of the dynamics part, the theory can be set as a benchmark
test for reactions with two charged particles in the ﬁnal channel. However, a similar
factorization of the transition amplitude involving ﬁnal states with three charged
particles was considered diﬃcult for a long time.
Our endeavor will be to show that by using a method based on plane wave
expansion [19, 20] of the distorted waves, it is still possible to resolve the transition
amplitude into the structure part and a modiﬁed dynamics part involving a sum
over a series of Bremsstrahlung integrals. This goes a long way in preserving the
analyticity of our approach. In the later part of our calculations we revisit the
problem of two charged particles in the ﬁnal state, using the above approach, and
see if we can put it on a ﬁrmer theoretical footing.
In section 6.2 we give details of the plane wave expansion method and its validity
for the Coulomb distorted waves. After giving the formalism in section 6.3 we
present our calculations for the case of three and two charged particles in the ﬁnal
states in subsection 6.3.1 and 6.3.2, respectively. Finally we conclude the result of
our calculations in section 6.4.

6.4

Conclusions

We have studied the problem of Coulomb breakup with three charged particles in
the ﬁnal channel in a post form reaction theory within the framework of the ﬁnite
range distorted wave Born approximation with plane wave expansion of Coulomb
distorted waves. Our calculations reveal that the breakup amplitude can be factorized into two parts - a structure part and a dynamics part, which in turn can be
expressed as a sum of a series of Bremsstrahlung integrals. Given the analyticity
of the method, applications of this theory to radiative capture reactions (especially
involving charged particles, like proton and alpha capture) as an indirect method in
astrophysics can be considered. It can also serve as a benchmark test for theories of
breakup reactions involving charged halo nuclei.
We have also calculated the breakup amplitude with two charged particles in
the ﬁnal channel, using our theory, and have shown that it can be put on a ﬁrmer
theoretical basis without invoking the local momentum approximation.

7.1

Summary

The work presented in this thesis concerns the application of the theory of elastic
Coulomb breakup of a projectile in the ﬁeld of a target nucleus under the framework
of ﬁnite range distorted wave Born approximation. The theory includes the electromagnetic interaction between the fragments and the target to all orders. Furthermore the breakup contribution from the entire non-resonant continuum (corresponding to all multipoles and relative orbital angular momentum between the fragments)
are also accounted for. The uncertainties associated with multipole strength distributions in many other formalisms are also avoided as one needs only the ground
state wave function of the projectile as an input. The analytic nature of this theory
stems from the fact that pure Coulomb wave functions are used in the calculation
and that the dynamics can be analytically evaluated. The application of the theory
to investigate the breakdown of N = 8 magic number, to calculate radiative capture cross sections and its subsequent extension to treat the breakup of a deformed
projectile is considered in the ﬁrst part of the thesis. This includes chapters 3 to

In chapter 3, we study the breakdown of N = 8 magic number near the neutron
drip line. We calculated the PMD of the charged fragment in the Coulomb breakup

post form ﬁnite range DWBA theory and the Adiabatic method. Indeed it has been

However, among the Be isotopes (N = 5, 6, 7, 8), 12 Be (N = 8) does not have the

the neutron drip line.

7.2.2

Need for a relativistic breakup theory

A further point of interest is the beam energy at which breakup reactions involving
exotic nuclei have been performed. Most of the ﬁrst generation experimental facilities (except at GSI) used to operate at beam energies less than 100 MeV/u. In the
recent upgrades beam energies around 350 MeV/u (RIKEN) and 1 GeV/u (GSI)
are common. So, if one is not satisﬁed in simply plugging in relativistic kinematics
into the non relativistic Schrödinger equation one could question the applicability
of the Schrödinger equation itself for such beam energies. Some initial thoughts in
this direction have already been proposed in Ref. [3].
So a relativistic description of the breakup process incorporating full Coulomb
(and nuclear) interactions between the fragments and the target, with ﬁnal state
interaction between breakup fragments and target nucleus taken to all orders (three
or four body ﬁnal states) is a theory that one would be looking for. Especially for
the Coulomb part, one can investigate if analytic or semi-analytic expressions for
the Coulomb amplitude can be derived, which could serve as benchmark for other
theories. A coherent addition of the Coulomb and nuclear amplitudes will enable
identiﬁcation the Coulomb-nuclear interference eﬀects. To the best of our knowledge
a systematic study of Coulomb-nuclear interference, especially in inelastic breakup,
has not been investigated before.

Open Quantum Systems (OQS)

It is also realised nowadays that intrinsically linked with the breakup process (more
so for exotic nuclei far from the valley of stability), is the knowledge that the eﬀect
of the continuum can no longer be neglected in understanding the stability of the
weakly bound systems. This has indeed opened a new theme of treating nuclei as
open quantum systems [1, 4], where one learns to work with non-Hermitian Hamiltonians. An interesting way forward is to use Random Matrix interactions [5, 6]
whereby properties of open and marginally stable quantum systems can be studied
using an eﬀective Hamiltonian with a complex part.
Open quantum systems are indeed new to nuclear physics and provides an opportunity to study non-compact algebras for the description of a system of interacting
fermions with bound and continuum parts in their energy spectrum. Calculations for
projectile breakup and particle transfers are topics one would like to explore within
the ambit of the OQS. This will also be of importance in nuclear astrophysics to
calculate cross sections of photodisintegration and radiative capture reactions.

We note that due to the presence of the interaction V (r1 ), an upper bound on the

In practical applications 1 , the vector K is evaluated at some ﬁxed distance r;
as is shown later on K(r) is almost constant after a certain value of r. Moreover
in peripheral collisions the nuclear interior contributes almost negligibly. In all our
calculations the local momentum is evaluated at r = 10 fm. The direction of the
local momentum vector is the only other thing that has to be speciﬁed. We take the
direction of K to be the same as that of the asymptotic momentum of the concerned
particle. It has been found that the cross sections are weakly dependent on the
directions of the local momentum, thereby justifying our choice of its direction.
